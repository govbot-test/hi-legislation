Section 1:
HB2408 HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES H.B. NO. 2408 THIRTY-THIRD LEGISLATURE, 2026 STATE OF HAWAII A BILL FOR AN ACT relating to FAMILY safety. BE IT ENACTED BY THE LEGISLATURE OF THE STATE OF HAWAII: PART I SECTION 1.  The purpose of this part is to designate the calendar year 2027 as the "Year of Resilience, Safety, and Survivor Justice". SECTION 2.  (a)  The calendar year 2027 shall be known and designated as the "Year of Resilience, Safety, and Survivor Justice". (b) During the calendar year 2027, the State commits to: (1)  Uplifting survivor voices; (2)  Strengthening family court practices; (3)  Improving community-based support systems; (4)  Investing in trauma-informed services; (5)  Expanding access to safety, legal protection, and healing; (6)  Advancing culturally grounded approaches; and (7)  Implementing the comprehensive reforms contained in this Act. (c) The State shall support public education, community partnerships, and survivor-led initiatives aligned with the Year of Resilience, Safety, and Survivor Justice. (d) State agencies may collaborate with domestic violence service providers, cultural practitioners, youth- and family-serving nonprofits organizations, and survivor-led organizations to advance awareness and prevention. PART II SECTION 3.  The legislature finds that Hawaii's statutory definitions of "harm", "child abuse or neglect", and related terms are outdated and fail to reflect modern science on trauma, psychological violence, coercive control, and non-visible injuries.  Abuse does not require visible injuries, and emotional harm can be more damaging than physical harm. The purpose of this part is to modernize statutory definitions to protect children from all forms of harm. SECTION 4.  Chapter 587A, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new section to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§587A-     Disability and neurodivergence non-justification.  (a)  A condition or injury shall not be considered justifiably explained nor shall maltreatment be excused, minimized, or dismissed on the basis of: (1)  A child's disability; (2)  Neurodivergence, autism, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, or developmental delay; (3)  A mental health diagnosis; (4)  Trauma-related or sensory-related behaviors; (5)  Behavioral challenges; or (6)  Communication differences. (b)  Disability or neurodivergence shall not justify: (1)  Physical force or restraint; (2)  Humiliation or degradation; (3)  Emotional maltreatment; or (4)  Isolation or coercion." SECTION 5.  Section 350-1, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by amending the definition of "child abuse or neglect" to read as follows: ""Child abuse or neglect" [means: (1)  The acts or omissions of any person who, or legal entity which, is in any manner or degree related to the child, is residing with the child, or is otherwise responsible for the child's care, that have resulted in the physical or psychological health or welfare of the child, who is under the age of eighteen, to be harmed, or to be subject to any reasonably foreseeable, substantial risk of being harmed.  The acts or omissions are indicated for the purposes of reports by circumstances that include but are not limited to: (A)  When the child exhibits evidence of: (i)  Substantial or multiple skin bruising or any other internal bleeding; (ii)  Any injury to skin causing substantial bleeding; (iii)  Malnutrition; (iv)  Failure to thrive; (v)  Burn or burns; (vi)  Poisoning; (vii)  Fracture of any bone; (viii)  Subdural hematoma; (ix)  Soft tissue swelling; (x)  Extreme pain; (xi)  Extreme mental distress; (xii)  Gross degradation; or (xiii)  Death; and such injury is not justifiably explained, or when the history given concerning such condition or death is at variance with the degree or type of such condition or death, or circumstances indicate that such condition or death may not be the product of an accidental occurrence; (B)  When the child has been the victim of sexual contact or conduct, including but not limited to sexual assault as defined in the Penal Code, molestation, sexual fondling, incest, or prostitution; obscene or pornographic photographing, filming, or depiction; or other similar forms of sexual exploitation, including but not limited to acts that constitute an offense pursuant to section 712-1202(1)(b); (C)  When there exists injury to the psychological capacity of a child as is evidenced by an observable and substantial impairment in the child's ability to function; (D)  When the child is not provided in a timely manner with adequate food, clothing, shelter, psychological care, physical care, medical care, or supervision; (E)  When the child is provided with dangerous, harmful, or detrimental drugs as defined by section 712-1240; provided that this subparagraph shall not apply when such drugs are provided to the child pursuant to the direction or prescription of a practitioner, as defined in section 712-1240; or (F)  When the child has been the victim of labor trafficking under chapter 707; or (2)  The acts or omissions of any person that have resulted in sex trafficking or severe forms of trafficking in persons; provided that no finding by the department pursuant to this chapter shall be used as conclusive evidence that a person has committed an offense under part VIII of chapter 707 or section 712-1202.] has the same meaning as in section 587A-4." SECTION 6.  Section 587A-4, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended as follows: 1. By adding a new definition to be appropriately inserted and to read: ""Child abuse or neglect" means any act by a parent, guardian, caregiver, custodian, household member, or any person responsible for the care or supervision of a child that causes, or is reasonably likely to cause, harm to the child's physical, emotional, psychological, developmental, or dignity-based well-being.  "Child abuse or neglect" does not require: (1)  Visible injury; (2)  Extreme pain; (3)  Bruising, fractures, or physical marks; (4)  Objectively observable impairment; (5)  Medical confirmation of injury; or (6)  Physical evidence of harm. "Child abuse or neglect" may consist of a pattern of behavior or a single severe incident." 2. By amending the definition of "harm" to read: ""Harm" [has the same meaning as "child abuse or neglect" as defined in section 350-1.] means any damage or injury to a child's physical, emotional, psychological, developmental, or dignity-based well-being, including but not limited to: (1)  Physical harm, whether or not visible; (2)  Emotional or psychological harm, including intimidation, humiliation, manipulation, isolation, or coercion; (3)  Exposure to domestic abuse or coercive control; (4)  Neglect or deprivation, excluding poverty alone; (5)  Sexual harm, including exploitation or grooming; (6)  Digital or online exploitation; (7)  Commercial exploitation; or (8)  Endangerment that creates a substantial risk of harm." PART III SECTION 7.  The legislature finds that coercive control is a leading predictor of lethal violence and must be recognized under state law.  Existing state law focuses on physical acts, leaving victims of non-physical abuse without protection. The purpose of this part is to define coercive control and integrate this definition into the domestic violence and child protection statutes. SECTION 8.  Chapter 586, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new part to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "Part    .  COERCIVE control and domestic violence modernization §586-     Coercive control as domestic abuse. Coercive control shall constitute domestic abuse for purposes of petitions for temporary restraining orders, protective orders, custody determinations, and all forms of family-court relief.  No physical injury shall be required. §586-     Duties of the court.  In addition to any other duties or responsibilities of the court, the court shall: (1)  Identify and make findings on coercive control whenever raised; (2)  Prioritize child safety and adult victim safety; (3)  Consider coercive control as a factor against custody, visitation, and shared decision-making; (4)  Not penalize parents for leaving an abuser or protecting a child; and (5)  Consider litigation abuse as domestic abuse. §586-     Training requirements.  (a) The following individuals shall receive training pursuant to subsection (b): (1)  Judges; (2)  Guardians ad litem; (3)  Custody evaluators; (4)  Child welfare services workers; (5)  Law enforcement officers; (6)  Mediators; and (7)  Court-appointed professionals. (b) The individuals listed in subsection (a) shall receive the following training: (1)  Trauma dynamics; (2)  Coercive control; (3)  Emotional and psychological abuse; (4)  Disability and neurodivergence issues; (5)  Litigation abuse; (6)  Financial abuse; (7)  Effects on children; (8)  System-based abuse; and (9)  Digital abuse." SECTION 9.  Chapter 587A, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new section to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§587A-     Coercive control.  (a) A child's exposure to coercive control shall constitute harm and child abuse or neglect, regardless of whether the child is physically touched. (b) For purposes of this section, "coercive control" has the same meaning as in section 586-1. SECTION 10.  Section 586-1, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by amending the definition of "coercive control" to read as follows: ""Coercive control" means a pattern of [threatening, humiliating, or intimidating actions, which may include assaults, or other abuse that is used to harm, punish, or frighten an individual.  "Coercive control" includes a pattern of behavior that seeks to take away the individual's liberty or freedom and strip away the individual's sense of self, including bodily integrity and human rights, whereby the "coercive control" is designed to make an individual dependent by isolating them from support, exploiting them, depriving them of independence, and regulating their everyday behavior including: (1)  Isolating the individual from friends and family; (2)  Controlling how much money is accessible to the individual and how it is spent; (3)  Monitoring the individual's activities, communications, and movements; (4)  Name-calling, degradation, and demeaning the individual frequently; (5)  Threatening to harm or kill the individual or a child or relative of the individual; (6)  Threatening to publish information or make reports to the police or the authorities; (7)  Damaging property or household goods; and (8)  Forcing the individual to take part in criminal activity or child abuse.] behavior, or a single severe incident, that unreasonably interferes with a person's autonomy, physical or emotional safety, or ability to maintain connections with others. "Coercive control" includes intimidation and threats; emotional and psychological abuse; isolation; monitoring, surveillance, and stalking; economic and financial abuse; reproductive and sexual coercion; litigation abuse, including legal, administrative, and system abuse; child manipulation and leverage; digital abuse; and disability or neurodivergence-related abuse." PART IV SECTION 11.  The legislature finds that family courts have allowed litigation abuse and pseudoscientific theories like parental alienation to harm children and protective parents. The purpose of this part is to prioritize child safety, prohibit parental alienation theories, and sanction parties who participate in abusive litigation tactics. SECTION 12.  Chapter 571, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding ten new sections to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§571-     Diagnosis shopping.  (a) Diagnosis shopping shall constitute coercive control, emotional abuse of the child, and system-based abuse of the protective parent. (b)  No court shall admit or rely upon any evaluation obtained through diagnosis shopping. (c)  Attorneys or evaluators who knowingly engage in or facilitate diagnosis shopping shall be subject to sanctions. §571-     Protective parent.  A protective parent shall not be penalized for: (1)  Seeking safety; (2)  Reporting abuse; (3)  Securing treatment for the child; or (4)  Limiting contact when the child is distressed or fearful. §571-     Prohibition on parental alienation.  (a) The concept of parental alienation, including its variants, shall not be recognized in the State. (b)  No court shall admit testimony, reports, evaluations, or evidence based on: (1)  Parental alienation; (2)  Parental alienation syndrome; (3)  Gatekeeping assumptions; (4)  Parental alignment theories; (5)  Family systems enmeshment used to discredit abuse; and (6)  Coaching allegations made without evidence. (c)  Any attempt by attorneys, evaluators, or parties to introduce parental-alienation-based theories shall result in: (1)  Exclusion of testimony; (2)  Fee shifting; (3)  Sanctions; (4)  Disqualification; and (5)  Referral to the office of disciplinary counsel. §571-     Child's right to refuse unsafe or distressing visitation.  (a)  A child's refusal to visit or spend time with a parent shall not result in penalties to the protective parent where: (1)  The child expresses fear, discomfort, trauma, or distress; (2)  There is evidence or concern of coercive control; (3)  The child witnessed abuse; (4)  The child has been the subject of emotional harm; (5)  The child's boundaries have been violated; or (6)  The child's developmental or emotional needs indicate distress. (b) Forcing visitation over the child's objection shall be prohibited. (c) Courts shall make written findings before any compelled visitation. §571-     Protective parent standard.  (a) A child shall not be removed from a protective parent except in extraordinary circumstances supported by clear and convincing evidence of: (1)  Immediate danger to the child; and (2)  Lack of reasonable protective alternatives. (b) A parent who acts to protect a child from abuse shall not be deemed uncooperative, obstructive, or alienating. §571-     Slanderous or abusive declarations; prohibited.  (a)  Courts shall strike any filing that contains: (1)  Humiliation, degradation, irrelevant personal or sexual information; (2)  Reproductive history; (3)  Medical shaming; (4)  Derogatory character attacks; (5)  Speculative accusations without evidence; or (6)  Abusive rhetoric. (b) Attorneys submitting such filings shall be subject to sanctions. §571-     Litigation abuse; remedies and sanctions.  (a) The court shall identify and sanction litigation abuse, including by: (1)  Fee shifting; (2)  Contempt; (3)  Limiting filings; (4)  Issuing protective orders; (5)  Ordering therapy or education for the abusive parent; (6)  Restricting custody or decision-making authority; (7)  Subjecting attorneys to sanctions; or (8)  Referring attorneys to the office of disciplinary counsel. §571-     Temporary restraining order; use against a parent; prohibited.  Dismissal of a temporary restraining order, whether voluntary, procedural, or evidentiary, shall not be used to: (1)  Penalize a parent; (2)  Assume false reporting; (3)  Assume malicious intent; or (4)  Alter custody without further findings. §571-     Judicial duties.  Judges shall: (1)  Consider coercive control in all custody cases; (2)  Make written findings where survivors allege abuse; (3)  Prioritize child emotional safety; (4)  Ensure children are not forced into unsafe contact; (5)  Reject pseudoscientific evidence; (6)  Recognize trauma behaviors; and (7)  Treat litigation abuse as domestic abuse. §571-     Mandatory training.  (a) The following shall receive training pursuant to subsection (b): (1)  Judges; (2)  Guardians ad litem; (3)  Custody evaluators; (4)  Child welfare services workers; (5)  Mediators; and (6)  Court-appointed therapists. (b)  The individuals listed in subsection (a) shall receive the following training: (1)  Coercive control; (2)  Trauma science; (3)  Litigation abuse; (4)  Child development; (5)  Disability and neurodivergence; (6)  Diagnosis shopping; (7)  Emotional and psychological harm; (8)  How abusers manipulate systems." SECTION 13.  Section 571-2, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding five new definitions to be appropriately inserted and to read as follows: ""Court-facilitated abuse" means any situation in which judicial processes, orders, procedures, delays, filings, or failure to act allow, enable, assist, or amplify abuse, coercive control, or harm to a child or protective parent. "Court-facilitated abuse" includes but is not limited to: (1)  Minimizing or dismissing coercive control; (2)  Ignoring patterns of psychological or emotional abuse; (3)  Compelling a child to have contact with an abusive parent; (4)  Penalizing a protective parent for respecting a child's trauma; (5)  Reliance on pseudoscientific theories, including parental alienation; (6)  Failure to sanction litigation abuse; (7)  Ordering reunification with an abusive parent; (8)  Ignoring financial abuse; (9)  Accepting abusive declarations or degrading submissions; (10)  Treating abuse as high conflict; and (11)  Failing to consider emotional safety. "Diagnosis shopping" means seeking, pressuring, coercing, influencing, or manipulating any medical, psychological, psychiatric, developmental, educational, or behavioral professional to obtain a diagnosis, label, assessment, or characterization of a child or parent that: (1)  Is intended to justify physical force, humiliation, coercive control, or harmful treatment; (2)  Misattributes trauma responses or fear-based behaviors to purported disability; (3)  Is sought to manipulate custody, visitation, or child welfare services involvement; (4)  Is used to discredit, undermine, or punish a protective parent; (5)  Is inconsistent with legitimate clinical standards; (6)  Is used to pathologize normal trauma reactions; or (7)  Is used to override the child's expressed fear, trauma, or distress. "Litigation abuse" means misuse of court procedures to harass, intimidate, exhaust, manipulate, dominate, financially harm, or discredit another party, including but not limited to: (1)  Repeated or frivolous motions; (2)  Excessive discovery; (3)  Refusal to comply with orders; (4)  Abusive declarations; (5)  Evidence manipulation; (6)  Subpoenas used to intimidate; (7)  Delay tactics; (8)  Filing complaints or reports in bad faith; (9)  Cross-filing temporary restraining orders or petitions to intimidate; and (10)  Threats of litigation to force concessions. "Litigation abuse" constitutes domestic abuse and coercive control. "Protective parent" means a legal parent who acts in good faith to protect a child from harm, including by respecting the child's expression of fear, trauma, or refusal of visitation. "Slanderous or abusive court submissions" includes: (1)  Filings containing humiliation, degradation, irrelevant sexual or reproductive information, private medical history, or character attacks; (2)  Filings intended to shame, intimidate, punish, or publicly humiliate; (3)  Defamatory statements offered without evidence; and (4)  Abusive declarations used to manipulate court perception." SECTION 14.  Section 571-46, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by amending subsection (a) to read as follows: "(a)  In actions for divorce, separation, annulment, separate maintenance, or any other proceeding where there is at issue a dispute as to the custody of a minor child, the court, during the pendency of the action, at the final hearing, or any time during the minority of the child, may make an order for the custody of the minor child as may seem necessary or proper. In awarding the custody, the court shall be guided by the following standards, considerations, and procedures: (1)  Custody should be awarded to either parent or to both parents according to the best interests of the child, [and the court also may consider frequent, continuing, and meaningful contact of each parent with the child] unless the court finds that a parent is unable to act in the best interest of the child; (2)  Custody may be awarded to persons other than the father or mother whenever the award serves the best interest of the child. Any person who has had de facto custody of the child in a stable and wholesome home and is a fit and proper person shall be entitled prima facie to an award of custody; (3)  If a child is of sufficient age and capacity to reason, so as to form an intelligent preference, the child's wishes as to custody shall be considered and be given due weight by the court; (4)  Whenever good cause appears therefor, the court may require an investigation and report concerning the care, welfare, and custody of any minor child of the parties.  When so directed by the court, investigators or professional personnel attached to or assisting the court, hereinafter referred to as child custody evaluators, shall make investigations and reports that shall be made available to all interested parties and counsel before hearing, and the reports may be received in evidence if no objection is made and, if objection is made, may be received in evidence; provided the person or persons responsible for the report are available for cross-examination as to any matter that has been investigated; and provided further that the court shall define, in accordance with section 571-46.4, the requirements to be a court-appointed child custody evaluator, the standards of practice, ethics, policies, and procedures required of court-appointed child custody evaluators in the performance of their duties for all courts, and the powers of the courts over child custody evaluators to effectuate the best interests of a child in a contested custody dispute pursuant to this section.  Where there is no child custody evaluator available that meets the requirements and standards, or any child custody evaluator to serve indigent parties, the court may appoint a person otherwise willing and available in accordance with section 571-46.4; (5)  The court may hear the testimony of any person or expert, produced by any party or upon the court's own motion, whose skill, insight, knowledge, or experience is such that the person's or expert's testimony is relevant to a just and reasonable determination of what is for the best physical, mental, moral, and spiritual well-being of the child whose custody is at issue; (6)  Any custody award shall be subject to modification or change whenever the best interests of the child require or justify the modification or change and, wherever practicable, the same person who made the original order shall hear the motion or petition for modification of the prior award; (7)  Reasonable visitation rights shall be awarded to parents, grandparents, siblings, and any person interested in the welfare of the child in the discretion of the court, unless it is shown that rights of visitation are detrimental to the best interests of the child; (8)  The court may appoint a guardian ad litem to represent the interests of the child and may assess the reasonable fees and expenses of the guardian ad litem as costs of the action, payable in whole or in part by either or both parties as the circumstances may justify; (9)  In every proceeding where there is at issue a dispute as to the custody of a child, a determination by the court that family violence has been committed by a parent raises a rebuttable presumption that it is detrimental to the child and not in the best interest of the child to be placed in sole custody, joint legal custody, or joint physical custody with the perpetrator of family violence.  In addition to other factors that a court shall consider in a proceeding in which the custody of a child or visitation by a parent is at issue, and in which the court has made a finding of family violence by a parent: (A)  The court shall consider as the primary factor the safety and well-being of the child and of the parent who is the victim of family violence; (B)  The court shall consider the perpetrator's history of causing physical harm, bodily injury, or assault or causing reasonable fear of physical harm, bodily injury, or assault to another person; and (C)  If a parent is absent or relocates because of an act of family violence by the other parent, the absence or relocation shall not be a factor that weighs against the parent in determining custody or visitation; (10)  A court may award visitation to a parent who has committed family violence only if the court finds that adequate provision can be made for the physical safety and psychological well-being of the child and for the safety of the parent who is a victim of family violence; (11)  In a visitation order, a court may: (A)  Order an exchange of a child to occur in a protected setting; (B)  Order visitation supervised by another person or agency; (C)  Order the perpetrator of family violence to attend and complete, to the satisfaction of the court, a program of intervention for perpetrators or other designated counseling as a condition of the visitation; (D)  Order the perpetrator of family violence to abstain from possession or consumption of alcohol or controlled substances during the visitation and for twenty-four hours preceding the visitation; (E)  Order the perpetrator of family violence to pay a fee to defray the costs of supervised visitation; (F)  Prohibit overnight visitation; (G)  Require a bond from the perpetrator of family violence for the return and safety of the child.  In determining the amount of the bond, the court shall consider the financial circumstances of the perpetrator of family violence; (H)  Impose any other condition that is deemed necessary to provide for the safety of the child, the victim of family violence, or other family or household member; and (I)  Order the address of the child and the victim to be kept confidential; (12)  The court may refer but shall not order an adult who is a victim of family violence to attend, either individually or with the perpetrator of the family violence, counseling relating to the victim's status or behavior as a victim as a condition of receiving custody of a child or as a condition of visitation; (13)  If a court allows a family or household member to supervise visitation, the court shall establish conditions to be followed during visitation; (14)  A supervised visitation center shall provide a secure setting and specialized procedures for supervised visitation and the transfer of children for visitation and supervision by a person trained in security and the avoidance of family violence; (15)  The court may include in visitation awarded pursuant to this section visitation by electronic communication provided that the court shall additionally consider the potential for abuse or misuse of the electronic communication, including the equipment used for the communication, by the person seeking visitation or by persons who may be present during the visitation or have access to the communication or equipment; whether the person seeking visitation has previously violated a temporary restraining order or protective order; and whether adequate provision can be made for the physical safety and psychological well-being of the child and for the safety of the custodial parent; (16)  The court may set conditions for visitation by electronic communication under paragraph (15), including visitation supervised by another person or occurring in a protected setting.  Visitation by electronic communication shall not be used to: (A)  Replace or substitute an award of custody or physical visitation except where: (i)  Circumstances exist that make a parent seeking visitation unable to participate in physical visitation, including military deployment; or (ii)  Physical visitation may subject the child to physical or extreme psychological harm; or (B)  Justify or support the relocation of a custodial parent; and (17)  Notwithstanding any provision to the contrary, no natural parent shall be granted custody of or visitation with a child if the natural parent has been convicted in a court of competent jurisdiction in any state of rape or sexual assault and the child was conceived as a result of that offense; provided that: (A)  A denial of custody or visitation under this paragraph shall not affect the obligation of the convicted natural parent to support the child; (B)  The court may order the convicted natural parent to pay child support; (C)  This paragraph shall not apply if subsequent to the date of conviction, the convicted natural parent and custodial natural parent cohabitate and establish a mutual custodial environment for the child; and (D)  A custodial natural parent may petition the court to grant the convicted natural parent custody and visitation denied pursuant to this paragraph, and upon such petition the court may grant custody and visitation to the convicted natural parent where it is in the best interest of the child." PART V SECTION 15.  The legislature finds that economic abuse is pervasive and destabilizes survivors and children.  Existing law lacks adequate safeguards against financial coercion during separation and custody disputes. The purpose of this part is to define economic abuse, strengthen automatic restraining orders, impose liability on financial institutions that enable abuse, and treat economic abuse as a factor in custody determinations. SECTION 16.  Chapter 412, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new section to article 1 to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§412:1- Protection against financial institution participation in economic abuse.  (a) It shall be unlawful for any financial institution to: (1)  Close, freeze, restrict, or alter an account based on unilateral requests from a spouse or partner; (2)  Remove a customer's name from a joint account without consent; (3)  Modify online access, passwords, or security settings without verification; (4)  Rely on information provided by an abusing party; (5)  Shut down accounts as retaliation for disclosures of domestic abuse; or (6)  Refuse service or deny reinstatement when coercive control is alleged. (b)  If an account is improperly altered, closed, or restricted, the institution shall: (1)  Restore the account to active status within five business days; (2)  Restore all prior features; (3)  Remove adverse credit reporting; (4)  Waive penalties or fees; and (5)  Provide written confirmation of reinstatement. (c)  Victims may sue a financial institution alleged to have committed a violation under this section for: (1)  Actual damages; (2)  Statutory damages ($2,500 per violation); (3)  Attorney's fees; (4)  Punitive damages in cases of wilful misconduct; and (5)  Injunctive relief. (d)  The department of commerce and consumer affairs may impose administrative fines of up to $10,000 per violation of this section. SECTION 17.  Chapter 580, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new section to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§580-     Automatic restraining order.  (a) Upon filing of a divorce, separation, or custody action, both parties shall be immediately restrained from: (1)  Closing, restricting, or modifying access to accounts; (2)  Withdrawing funds other than for ordinary living expenses; (3)  Creating new debt secured by marital assets; (4)  Changing passwords or online banking access; (5)  Interfering with direct deposit; (6)  Altering insurance or beneficiary designations; (7)  Removing names from accounts; (8)  Unilaterally capturing or withholding rental income or other recurring income historically used for household or child support, except for ordinary living expenses or by court order; and (9)  Taking any action that destabilizes the victim or child. (b)  Any violation of this section shall result in: (1)  Immediate restitution; (2)  Fee shifting; (3)  Potential adverse property division; (4)  Contempt findings; or (5)  Sanctions." SECTION 18.  Chapter 586, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding five new sections to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§586-     Economic abuse.  (a)  Upon sworn allegations of economic abuse, the court shall schedule a hearing within seven to fourteen days. (b)  The court may issue ex parte financial freeze orders to: (1)  Prevent asset dissipation; (2)  Prevent unauthorized withdrawals; (3)  Ensure access to essentials; or (4)  Maintain the status quo. §586-     Forensic accounting authority.  Courts may: (1)  Appoint forensic accountants; (2)  Order expedited financial disclosures; (3)  Subpoena records; and (4)  Charge forensic costs to the abusing party. §586-     Emergency economic abuse hearings.  Upon sworn allegations of economic abuse, the court shall schedule a hearing within fourteen days and may issue ex parte financial freeze orders to prevent asset dissipation and ensure access to essentials. §586-     Economic abuse as a factor in custody determinations.  Economic abuse shall constitute domestic abuse under section 571-46 and shall be a negative factor that weighs against awarding custody, unsupervised visitation, and joint decision-making. §586-     Penalties for assisting in economic abuse; mandatory training.  (a)  Attorneys who knowingly assist in economic abuse, including through concealed asset transfers or abusive financial tactics, shall be subject to fee shifting, contempt, sanctions, disqualification, and referral to the office of disciplinary counsel. (b)  Mandatory training for judges, guardians ad litem, custody evaluators, and court-appointed professionals shall include identification of economic abuse, financial trauma, and forensic indicators." SECTION 19.  Section 586-1, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding a new definition to be appropriately inserted and to read as follows: ""Economic abuse" means a pattern of behavior, or a single severe incident, that controls, obstructs, restricts, or sabotages a person's access to financial resources, economic stability, property, employment, credit, or ability to meet basic needs.  "Economic abuse" includes: (1)  Financial control and deprivation; (2)  Financial sabotage; (3)  Misuse of financial institutions; (4)  Economic threats; (5)  Coerced financial dependence; (6)  Economic abuse affecting children; and (7)  Litigation-related economic abuse." PART VI SECTION 20.  The legislature finds that neurodivergent children and those falsely labeled as such face heightened risk of harm and discrimination.  Disability or diagnosis must never justify abuse or be weaponized in custody disputes. The purpose of this part is to prohibit misuse of disability, ban diagnosis shopping, require trauma-informed evaluations, and protect protective parents from retaliation. SECTION 21.  Chapter 587A, Hawaii Revised Statutes, is amended by adding eight new sections to be appropriately designated and to read as follows: "§587A-     Protective advocacy.  A parent's actions to seek accommodations, therapy, support, trauma-informed services, or evaluations that serve the best interest of the child shall not be treated as obstruction, alienation, or instability. §587A-     Disability or neurodivergence non-justification.  (a)  No child's disability, neurodivergence, developmental differences, mental health diagnosis, or trauma-related behavior, actual or alleged, shall be used as justification or excuse for: (1)  Physical force or restraint; (2)  Seclusion, isolation, or confinement; (3)  Humiliating or degrading treatment; (4)  Emotional or psychological abuse; (5)  Threats or intimidation; (6)  Coercive control; (7)_ Excessive or punitive discipline; or (8)  Withholding comfort, support, therapy, or safety. PART VII SECTION 23.  The legislature finds that reproductive autonomy is a fundamental right under the Hawaii State Constitution.  Abusers weaponize reproductive history, including abortion, miscarriage, infertility treatment, contraception, and pregnancy outcomes, during family court proceedings to shame, intimidate, and discredit parents.  Such practices constitute coercive control and litigation abuse and have no legitimate relevance to parental fitness. The purpose of this part is to prohibit the introduction of reproductive history in custody disputes, impose sanctions for violations, and provide remedies for affected parties. (b)  All members of the family violence advisory panel shall be appointed by the governor pursuant to section 26-34.  The family violence advisory panel shall consist of the following five members: (1)  One representative from a Hawaii-based nonprofit domestic violence advocacy organization; (2)  One representative from a Hawaii-based nonprofit specializing in child and youth trauma; (3)  One licensed mental health professional with expertise in trauma and family violence; (4)  One indigenous or cultural practitioner with expertise in culturally grounded trauma-informed practice; and (5)  One survivor of domestic violence or protective parent with lived experience. (c) The family violence advisory panel shall: (1)  Assist in designing and updating required judicial training; (2)  Review statewide data to identify systemic safety issues; (3)  Recommend improvements to trauma-informed court procedures; (4)  Identify harmful evaluator practices; (5)  Provide culturally grounded insight; and (6)  Publish an annual report to the governor, legislature, and judiciary. (d) The family violence advisory panel may request anonymized data, recommend corrective training, and propose best-practice reforms. (e) The survivor-member shall receive confidentiality protections, remote participation options, trauma-informed accommodations. (f)  The family violence advisory panel shall serve without compensation but shall be reimbursed for expenses, including travel expenses, necessary for the performance of their duties. (g) The judiciary shall allocate funds for panel operations and compensation. §571-H  Prohibition on untrained judges hearing family violence cases.  (a)  No judge shall preside over any family violence case without completing required training. (b) Orders issued in violation of this section shall be voidable and constitute grounds for appeal. §571-I  Data collection and oversight.  (a) The judiciary shall compile and publish annual data including: (1)  Number of family violence cases; (2)  Assignment compliance; (3)  Judicial training status; (4)  Child safety outcomes; (5)  Use of mediation or reunification orders; and (6)  Evaluator and guardian ad litem usage patterns. (b)  An annual report of the information in subsection (a) shall be submitted to the legislature no later than twenty days prior to the convening of each regular session." PART XI SECTION 33.  If any provision of this Act, or the application thereof to any person or circumstance, is held invalid, the invalidity does not affect other provisions or applications of the Act that can be given effect without the invalid provision or application, and to this end the provisions of this Act are severable. SECTION 34.  This Act does not affect rights and duties that matured, penalties that were incurred, and proceedings that were begun before its effective date. SECTION 35.  In codifying the new sections added by section 32 of this Act, the revisor of statutes shall substitute appropriate section numbers for the letters used in designating the new sections in this Act. SECTION 36.  Statutory material to be repealed is bracketed and stricken.  New statutory material is underscored. SECTION 37.  This Act shall take effect on July 1, 2026.
